The body loses more than we realize during illness.

Hospitalization, mechanical ventilation, and aging all trigger a common pathway: rapid, progressive muscle loss. Patients can lose up to 2% of muscle mass per day in the ICU.

  • ✓Up to 33% of ICU patients develop acquired weakness

The science

Creatine and the phosphocreatine energy shuttle

Creatine plays a central role in cellular energy metabolism — specifically in the rapid regeneration of ATP, the body's primary energy currency. In states of muscle catabolism, creatine supplementation may attenuate protein degradation and support recovery at the cellular level.

Muscle loss

What illness and aging take from you.

Sarcopenia accelerates dramatically during hospitalization. For critically ill patients on mechanical ventilation, up to 2% of lean muscle mass can be lost per day.

Muscle is the organ of longevity. Its decline correlates directly with functional independence, fall risk, cognitive resilience, and mortality.

Mechanism 01

Catabolic signaling cascade

Illness activates proteolytic pathways that systematically break down muscle protein. Inflammatory cytokines accelerate this process, creating a negative protein balance even with adequate caloric intake.

Mechanism 02

Mitochondrial dysfunction

Critical illness impairs mitochondrial biogenesis and electron transport chain efficiency. Less ATP is produced per unit of substrate, reducing the cell's capacity for both contraction and repair.

Mechanism 03

Neuromuscular disuse atrophy

Immobilization silences motor neuron input to muscle fibers. Without trophic signals, fiber cross-sectional area declines rapidly — independent of nutritional status.

ATP — your body's energy currency

Every movement runs on ATP.

Every movement, every heartbeat, every cellular repair process requires adenosine triphosphate (ATP). Your body produces and uses approximately its own body weight in ATP every single day.1

As we age, mitochondrial efficiency declines. This means less ATP is available for muscle contraction, tissue repair, and recovery.2,3

The biochemistry of recovery
The ATP-CP system

Your body's fastest energy pathway

The ATP-CP system is anaerobic and instantaneous. When you stand from a chair or catch your balance, your muscles rely almost exclusively on this system for the first 10-15 seconds of effort.2,4

Metabolic buffering

Preventing fatigue accumulation

Creatine facilitates rapid ADP-to-ATP recycling, preventing byproduct accumulation that causes muscle fatigue. In clinical recovery, this allows patients to complete physical therapy with less exertion.1,4

Mitochondrial support

Bypassing the aging bottleneck

The creatine shuttle moves energy from mitochondria to muscle fibers. Supplementation helps bypass mitochondrial bottlenecks that occur during aging or chronic illness.1,5

Neuro-metabolic protection

Supporting cognitive recovery too

The phosphocreatine system is vital for maintaining cognitive function during metabolic stress — supporting the neurological drive required for motor control and rehabilitation.3

Study design

ICU creatine supplementation trial

Mechanically ventilated patients receive clinical-grade creatine monohydrate versus standard nutritional care. Functional assessments at discharge, 30 days, and 90 days post-extubation.

Why this population

The gap in critical care nutrition

ICU patients represent the most dramatic case of acute muscle loss. If creatine preserves function in this extreme context, its applicability to aging adults, post-surgical patients, and rehabilitation populations is strongly supported.
